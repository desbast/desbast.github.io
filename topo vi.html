3d-printed island [WIP]

The following maps were generated as a part of a large-print-series. For sale are large cartographic prints spanning a specific section of topography in the Pacific North-West. The source data was extracted from a Digital Elevation Model (DEM) database maintained by the government of British Columbia. In all, 178 DEM scan files were sourced to compile Vancouver Island, and then stitched together using a mosaicking tool within a mapping analytics software. The master DEM now created from the 178 scans was then put through a sink fill process, removing any smaller dataset imperfections. The result of this was then put through a flow-direction render, assigning values to individual data cells pertaining to their respective steepest downslope neighbor. This output was then fed through a flow accumulation process, rendering a rather beautiful raster that indicates accumulation points. The accumulation raster was then ran through a single output map-algebra equation that defined the river network as having a 3000 flow accumulation unit cut-off. This could have ranged any number between 0-10000, however in this case, 3000 made the most sense visually to delineate between ocean and river. This was probably the most tedious selection to make in the process, as Vancouver Island is quite porous, with many inlets blurring the lines between river and ocean. The output was then classified to a 6 part Strahler stream order, separating the assumed flows into categories that can then descend visually. This was then converted into vector graphics, ensuring a tidy print made up of operable components. WIP: a client has requested a mounted 3d-print of Vancouver Island. More on this to come!
